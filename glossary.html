  • +1d/-1d: Add or remove one six-sided die (D6) from the Dice Pool of the roll. Bonus dice for Action Rolls can be obtained by making a Dark Bargain and by spending a single Dark Heart (pp. 62–64)
  • Action Dots / Action Rating: Determines how skilled a character is when performing certain types of Action. Action ratings don’t just represent skill or training—you’re free to describe how your character performs that action based on the type of person they are. When performing the associated action, a PC starts with the amount of D6 in his Dice Pool that corresponds to his rating (0 to 3). If the rating is 0 they roll two dice and take the worse result. The action ratings are: (Refer to practice xp, too) (pp. 10, 80)
  • Action Rolls: Central game mechanic. When a PC attempts an action that’s dangerous or troublesome, you make an action roll to find out how it goes. If no danger or trouble would result from a failure a Fortune Roll might be more appropriate. The player states their goal for the action and chooses the action rating. The GM sets the Position for the roll and the Effect Level for the action. bonus dice going hard items assist team up (pp. 26, 30–32, 36–39)

  • Attributes: Actions are broken up into three categories called attributes, indicating how the related actions are typically used. Attributes be affected by Shock, weakening their actions temporarily. The categories are Brains (Scan, Tinker, Trick), Muscles (Finesse, Skulk, Smash) and Guts (Banter, Invoke, Threaten) (p. 28)
  • Banter: Action under the Guts Attribute: When you banter, you affably get people to help you because they like you (p. 29)
  • Bloodied: Represents a significant injury, putting you just one step away from Death. While Shock is for painful but minor injuries, bloodied is for the big stuff. You immediately take shock to all Attributes and remain bloodied until all shock has been cleared. During this time, taking another equivalent wound kills you immediately - though death can also be Resisted (p. 44)

The callings are: Brute, Conniver, Crafter, Hunter, Marauder, Shadow, Shaman, Warlock and Zealot (pp. 88, 94–111)

If the pool size is zero or less, two dice are rolled and the lowest result counts. Downtime actions are performed with an Action Roll at Dominant Position, unless there is an overwhelming circumstance towards position. Players unhappy with the result may also Risk It to push their luck. (p. 238–242)
  • Downtime Projects: During the Lurking Phase after the Revelry, PCs get some time to work on downtime projects before their next Raid. Each PC takes one Downtime Action, they can also pay their Minions 1 Gold to undertake a downtime action on their behalf. PCs can gain further downtime actions by taking certain Special Abilities. Players can also Flashback to performing downtime actions that are immediately relevant to a Scene, Paying Cost to represent the strenuous effort. The Imps of the Dungeon also take one downtime action controlled by the GM and rolling the dungeon Tier Project Tier link (pp. 238–242)

  • Effect: The level at which one would achieve the goals set for an action. Reaching from zero and limited via normal and great to extreme. Contributing factors are the suitability of action rating involved in the situation at hand, as well as the factors of potency, scale and quality/tier which can increase and decrease the expected effect level of the outcome. Dominant factors can reduce the effect to zero. Impossible actions may result in zero effect even with characters pushing themselves and critical results on an action (pp. 36–39)

Starts with a dice pool of one with bonus dice being added or subtracted based on the operation, plan, detail, aid & interference, target tier and other factors such as set up actions or preparatory jobs and operations (p. 40)

  • Flashback: While working a job players can invoke a flashback to take an action in the past that impacts your current situation. A flashback isn’t time travel. It can’t “undo” something that just occurred. The GM sets a stress cost from 0 (ordinary action for which you had easy opportunity) to 2+ (elaborate action that involved special opportunities or contingencies). Handled like any other action. Might entail an action roll (if there’s danger or trouble), a fortune roll (determine the effect level) or no roll at all. If a flashback involves a downtime activity, pay 1 cred for it instead of stress (p. 65)

  • Forsaken: Zealots forsaken by their God lose access to Tier 2 and tier 3 spells until they have atoned in some way. The GM can also Compel them towards actions that would please their god. Those are essentially the opposite of the things that they can be forsaken for. If the compel is suppressed, their god immediately forsakes them (p. 150)

PCs can carry 4 items as gear. Players work with the GM to decide what defense their gear provides them. (pp. 70–72)

  • Gold: Gold is a measure of how much of the Dungeon's Hoard each PC controls individually. Gold can be spent at any point during Downtime or within Scenes, even using your Calling Flashbacks to spend it in the past. Gold is gained during Revelry as you sift through the Loot from your last Raid. Each PC can control up to 3 gold worth of the hoard. It’s called gold for simplicity, but it represents whatever your dungeon hoards (gems, old tomes, weapons ...) and the ability to get others to do things for you. Smaller amounts of wealth aren’t tracked. Gold can be spent to get help from a Vile Friend or Faction, Pay Cost, Pay Minions to perform a Downtime Action, pay minions to join a raid, Recruit a Minion Pack or attract a Creature or to Upgrade a minion pack (p. 73)

Each Downtime, the GM rolls one downtime action representing the imps effort within the dungeon. They roll the dungeon Tier for this. Imps only speak in grunts, squeeks, and gestures, though they can understand the Dark Tongue. They can only have very low impact on any situation, merely adding flavor to Scenes. If you ever need to determine an imp’s effectiveness, they roll 0d and take -Effect. (p. 187)

Without the appropriate Calling Core Ability they are limited to Tier 1 spells. Merely detecting, understanding, and even dispelling magic does not require a magic focus (pp. 29, 124, 128)
  • Items: Gear and Supply like Weapons, Armor, Magic Items, Concotions, Potions, Contraptions or Other Valuables. Items fall into three categories: mundane, valuable (a little rare, maybe of Tier 1, or with an Edge or containing valuable information), and powerful (multiple edges or a tier 2 or 3 item, could be one of a kind or contains very secret information). When a PC buys, steals, Makes, or Acquires an item, it is added to their gear or supply. Items are sometimes considered as a circumstance towards Effect. If several items serve the same purpose, they can be combined into just one gear or supply (e.g. “armor” representing a helmet and shield). The bigger the impact a single item has, the more likely it should be listed on its own. (p. 70)

Each magic focus can only cast spells of a single magic path. They're usually related in some way to their path, though the exact choice of item is up to you. As long as you have a magic focus, any Wicked One can Invoke magic. However, you can only cast tier 1 spells without the appropriate calling core ability. (p. 124)

A dangerous NPC can even take the initiative. The GM describes what the NPC has done or is about to accomplish, then ask the character if they want to resist it (p. )

Once the plan is set in motion the engagement roll determines how much trouble the PCs are in when they reach the first obstacle. There are six different types of plan: Assault, Deception, Infiltration, Mystic, Social, Transport (p. )

If it seems less dangerous, make it controlled. Negotiate with your GM over the position you find yourself in and what you might do to improve it. Sometimes the position is risky for reasons that aren’t clear until you ask questions (p. 36-39)

  • Progress Clocks: Circles divided into 4–8 segments / ticks to track ongoing effort against an obstacle or approaching trouble. Clocks can be used to represent growing danger, races of several opposed parties, linked events, shrinking time windows for opportunities, tug-of-war efforts and long-term project progress. Usually limited effects progress a clock by one tick, standard by two and great effects by three -- Objective Clocks, Danger Clocks, Situation clocks, Racing clocks, Layered Clocks (pp. 66–68)
  • Project Tiers: Projects are rated from tier 1 to tier 3 according to how much effort they take to finish. This generally corresponds to the tier of what you're trying to make or is set by the GM according to the table below when it's unclear. Some projects are accomplishable with a single roll while others require you to fill a clock. Tier 1: 1 Roll (mixed --> pay cost, crit --> something extra) Tier 2: 4-segment clock Tier 3: 8-segment clock (pp. 238–239, 240–242)

  • Resistance Rolls: When your PC suffers a consequence that you don’t like, you can choose to resist it. Resistance is always automatically effective—the GM says if the consequence is reduced in severity or if you avoid it entirely. Make a resistance roll to see how much stress your character suffers as a result of their resistance. You make the roll using one of your character’s attributes (insight, prowess or resolve). The GM chooses the attribute, based on the nature of consequence. Your character suffers 6 stress minus the highest die result from the resistance roll. If you get a critical result, you also clear 1 stress (pp. 50–53)
  • Resisting-Death: Resisting Death has harsher rules. On a Mixed, the Consequence is reduced in severity but still incredibly serious and the GM should make clear what the consequences will be before you roll. On a failure, you still die. If you have Dark Hearts, perform your Death Knell (p. 52)

The result of the resistance roll determines how much stress it costs to avoid or mitigate a bad outcome. Stress can also be used to pay for assisting, flashbacks and pushing yourself.   • Supply: Things that PCs own or have access to, but might not always be carrying on them. Supply allows PCs to flexibly pull Items out of their inventory when needed. PCs get 2 supply boxes and when they pull something out of supply, players mark one of the boxes. When out of boxes, that means the PC isn’t carrying any other items. Supply boxes reset on the next Recovery. Any item in the supply must be able to be carried in one hand or fit nicely in a backpack. Items such as Concoctions, Potions, or anything else that is limited-use must always be placed in supply. A Steady Supply represents constant access to a consumable resource while a Shared Supply for the Dungeon means all members can access it as supply with a clock (usually 4-segments) tracking the usage (pp. 70–72)

Tiers determine their relative power, effectiveness, or challenge levels and can be considered as circumstances towards Position and Effect. Power roughly doubles with every tier. Dungeon Tier represents the size of your dungeon’s Hoard and how powerful your dungeon, the Wicked Ones, and the Minions have grown. Faction Tiers represent how powerful organized groups in the region are. Factions higher in tier than your dungeon affect your Engagement Rolls for any Raids against them. The tier also determines what kind of Blowback you might face from raiding that faction (p. 76)
